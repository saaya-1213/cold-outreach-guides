How to Write a Cold Email That Gets Replies

A 4-line framework you can use for any prospect. Updated 2026.

Most cold emails fail because they're about the sender. The ones that get replies are short, specific, and about the recipient. Here's the structure that works.

The 4-line framework

Line 1 — Relevance: the {trigger} that proves you looked
Line 2 — Bridge: connect that to a problem they likely have
Line 3 — Value: one sentence on how you help (no feature dump)
Line 4 — Soft ask: "worth a 10-min look?" (a yes/no question)

Example

Hi {name},
Saw {company} just launched {product} — congrats. Scaling outreach
without it feeling like spam is usually the next headache.
I help teams send personalized first emails + track replies without
a monthly tool. Worth a quick look this week?
{you}

The rules that make it work

  • 50–70 words. If it scrolls, it's too long.
  • One idea, one ask. Don't bundle three things.
  • One real detail. A {trigger} beats ten merge tags — see subject lines.
  • No link in email #1. It protects deliverability.
  • Follow up once, politely, with a new angle.

What "good" looks like

A dialed-in cold email earns a 5–15% reply rate. Below 3%? It's almost always the targeting or the relevance line — not the template.
