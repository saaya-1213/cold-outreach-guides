Cold Email Deliverability Checklist (2026)

Since late 2024, Gmail and Microsoft reject non-compliant bulk mail at the SMTP level. In 2026, deliverability matters more than features. Here's the full checklist.

The single biggest reason cold emails fail isn't the copy — it's that they never reach the inbox. Blasting thousands of messages from a fresh domain stopped working when Gmail and Microsoft tightened enforcement. Follow this checklist before you send a single email.

1. Authenticate your domain (non-negotiable)

  • SPF — authorizes which servers can send for your domain.
  • DKIM — cryptographically signs your mail.
  • DMARC — tells inboxes what to do with mail that fails the above.

All three are now effectively mandatory for bulk senders. Your email host's docs walk you through the DNS records in ~15 minutes.

2. Use a separate sending domain

Never send cold email from your primary domain. Buy a lookalike (e.g., get-yourbrand.com), so if it gets flagged, your main domain's reputation is safe.

3. Warm up slowly

A new domain should send ≤20 emails/day in week one, then ramp gradually over 2–4 weeks. Sudden volume spikes look exactly like spam.

4. Keep email #1 clean

  • No links and no images in the first email.
  • Plain text beats heavy HTML.
  • Short subject (≤5 words), no spam words (free, guarantee, $$$, !!!).
  • End with a question — replies teach inbox providers you're wanted.

5. Stay compliant

Always include a clear opt-out and a physical address (CAN-SPAM), and respect GDPR / local rules. Compliance also improves deliverability because it lowers spam complaints.
